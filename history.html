Current Impacts of Financial Struggles

Impacts on Justice-Involved Students

Author: Je'von

Access to higher education is one of the most effective pathways to upward mobility, stability, and long-term success. However, financial barriers continue to prevent many students, especially justice-involved individuals, from fully participating in college. This issue is deeply personal to me because, as a justice-involved student with limited resources, I face challenges that many traditional students do not experience. Understanding these barriers highlights why policy reform, institutional support, and expanded financial aid are critical for student success.

Financial Barriers Facing Justice-Involved Students

Students impacted by the justice system often encounter unique obstacles when attempting to access financial aid, housing, and campus resources. Research shows that policy restrictions, stigma, and eligibility limitations can reduce access to funding and institutional support[7][14]. Even when programs like federal aid exist, awareness gaps and complicated application processes prevent many students from applying successfully.

Additionally, rising tuition costs, technology expenses, transportation fees, and living costs place heavy burdens on students. Many students must work long hours to survive, which can negatively affect academic performance and persistence. For justice-involved students with limited financial networks or family support, these challenges are even more significant.

Click image to be taken to the article that hosts interactive version of the map.[14]

Housing, Food, and Basic Needs Insecurity

Basic needs insecurity, including housing instability and food insecurity, directly affects academic performance and graduation rates. When students worry about where they will live or how they will afford meals, it becomes difficult to focus on coursework. Rising living expenses, inflation, and insufficient financial aid packages widen the gap between available resources and actual cost of attendance. For students without stable income or family assistance, these barriers can make continuing education extremely difficult.

Stigma and Institutional Barriers

Justice-involved students also face stigma that can limit opportunities. Policies, background checks, and admissions processes may unintentionally exclude or discourage students with criminal records. According to research, these barriers can prevent students from accessing housing assistance, scholarships, and campus employment opportunities. This creates structural inequality that affects educational attainment and long-term economic mobility.

Conclusion

Financial struggles in higher education affect many students, but justice-involved individuals face additional systemic barriers that compound these challenges. Rising costs, limited aid access, stigma, and basic needs insecurity all impact academic success. Addressing these issues through policy reform, expanded financial support, and campus resources is essential for creating equitable educational opportunities. Higher education should serve as a pathway forward, not a barrier, especially for students seeking to rebuild and create a better future.

 

Financial Stress & Student Mental Health

Author: Ashley

Financial Worries and Psychological Distress

Financial insecurity increases anxiety and psychological distress among adults. Persistent financial stress is linked to serious physical and mental health consequences[13].

The study demonstrates that individuals experiencing economic instability report higher levels of mental health challenges and long-term stress outcomes[13].

Financial Stress in College Students

Financial stress impacts academic performance and social relationships.

Students experience:

  • Difficulty affording materials.
  • Prioritizing employment over academics.
  • Social comparison and exclusion.
  • Shame and class-based barriers.

Some students form supportive peer communities, but universities should reduce financial barriers to improve student success[12].

Debt and Financial Knowledge

Research on financial counseling shows that student loans and credit card debt increase anxiety and stress levels[1].

Students who reported greater financial satisfaction experienced approximately 30% lower worry levels compared to those with low financial confidence[1].

Financial literacy reduces stress because students who understand budgeting, loans, and interest rates feel more confident managing their finances[1].

Key recommendations include:

  1. Building financial awareness.
  2. Increasing financial literacy education.
  3. Seeking counseling and financial support.

Mental Health & Crisis Support

Clark College provides counseling and crisis support services to support student mental health and well-being[3].

Students experiencing crisis can access the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline, which provides 24/7 support and specialized services for veterans, Spanish speakers, and Deaf/Hard of Hearing individuals.

Research shows that financial insecurity increases anxiety, depression, and long-term psychological distress. Economic stress is associated with serious physical and emotional consequences, including increased health risks[13].

Financial stress among college students affects academic performance and social belonging. Students report difficulty affording textbooks, prioritizing work over school, and experiencing exclusion due to financial barriers[12].

Academic Support Services

Clark College provides tutoring and academic coaching services to support student success[6][4].

Tutoring services assist students in subjects such as math, English, science, business, and technology.

Academic coaching improves time management, study skills, and academic planning, which is especially important because financial stress negatively affects concentration and retention[12][2].

Basic Needs Support

Basic needs resources include food assistance, emergency grants, housing support, transportation support, and childcare assistance.

More than one-third of college students struggle to afford basic needs, and financial hardship reduces graduation rates[10].

Rising tuition and indirect costs such as housing, books, and transportation increase financial pressure on students. Colleges are expanding basic needs programs to improve retention and reduce financial barriers[10].

 

Housing

Author: Kristin

Even though Clark College offers strong support in many areas, there is no dedicated housing or rental assistance program specifically for Clark students. Students must rely on community-wide resources, which can be difficult to navigate, competitive, or not tailored to student schedules and income patterns.

The Gap

  • Clark has no student housing (on-campus or off-campus partnerships).
  • There is no student-specific rental assistance, only general emergency grants.
  • Students are referred to community agencies, which serve the entire county and often have long waitlists.
  • Housing instability is one of the top barriers to college persistence, especially for community college students.
  • Vancouver's rental market is expensive, and many students work part-time or rely on financial aid.

What Clark Does Offer

  • Basic Needs Hub (referrals only)
  • Emergency Grants (limited, short-term)
  • Workforce Education Services (helps some students, but not housing-specific)
  • Counseling support (guidance, not housing)

These are helpful, but none of them are housing programs.

Ways Clark College Could Improve Housing & Rental Support

  1. Create a Home Sharing Program
    • A home sharing program like the one at San Mateo Community College “connects community members who have a spare room or an Accessory Dwelling Unit (“Providers”) with individuals seeking affordable housing (“Seekers”). Matches may be arranged as a standard Rent Exchange or a Reduced Rent Exchange, in which lower rent is paired with help around the home”[11].
    • This gives students a safe place to stay for a few nights while they stabilize.
  2. Public or Private Partnership
    • A long-term option could be for Clark College to create student-specific housing, like affordable micro-apartments or a mixed-use building designed for student life. Other community colleges have already done this through public-private partnerships, which keeps costs manageable while still giving students safe, stable, low-cost places to live. Having dedicated student housing would directly fill the gap we identified by giving students reliable, student-centered living options that support their stability, academic success, and overall well-being. An example of this kind of partnershipcan be found here.
  3. Hire or designate a Housing Navigator
    • A staff member trained to help students find housing, apply for programs, and understand their options, similar to a financial aid advisor, but for housing stability.
    • Encourage landlords to reserve a small number of units for students.
    • Negotiate student-friendly lease terms or reduced deposits.
    • Helps students safely find roommates, reduces rent by splitting costs, and could include safety guidelines and optional background checks.
    • One place where students can find rentals, roommate matching, emergency help, and local programs.
    • Clear, updated info instead of sending students to scattered community resources.
